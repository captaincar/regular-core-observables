A Radical Idea About Black Holes, Dark Matter, and Why the Universe Exists

The short version

A pair of physicists asked a strange question: what if the laws of relativity treated observers who travel faster than light on the same footing as the rest of us? Their answer — that this naturally gives you something that looks suspiciously like quantum mechanics — is provocative enough on its own. But a new computational analysis asks an even bolder follow-up: could this same idea also explain what's inside a black hole, what dark matter is made of, and why there's more matter than antimatter in the universe? The answer, so far, is a cautious "maybe someday, but not yet." Along the way, however, the calculations have produced three concrete, testable predictions — one of which is already constrained by gravitational-wave data that exists today.

The problem, in plain English

Black holes are the universe's best-kept secrets. Once you cross the event horizon, the roles of space and time swap: moving toward the center becomes as inevitable as moving toward tomorrow. Einstein's equations handle this just fine — right up until they don't. At the center, the math blows up into an infinity. Everyone agrees this means Einstein's theory is incomplete there, but nobody agrees on what replaces it.

Meanwhile, cosmology has two other enormous holes. Dark matter: something out there has gravity but doesn't glow, and we have no idea what it is. And baryogenesis: for every billion antiparticles in the early universe, there were roughly a billion-and-one particles — and that tiny surplus is why anything exists at all. Standard physics struggles to explain why.

Enter Andrzej Dragan and Artur Ekert, two physicists who in 2020 published a paper with a wonderfully audacious premise. They asked: what happens if you refuse to throw away the faster-than-light solutions that naturally pop out of relativity's equations? Their answer: you get non-determinism, multiple histories, and complex probability amplitudes — the mathematical machinery of quantum theory — all from relativity alone. No quantum mechanics required; it just falls out.

The big idea (without the equations)

Here's the intuition. In ordinary relativity, nothing can travel faster than light. But the math itself doesn't actually forbid superluminal motion — it just says that if it happened, cause and effect would get scrambled from different observers' perspectives. A superluminal signal that looks like emission-before-absorption to one observer would look like absorption-before-emission to another.

Dragan and Ekert's key move was to say: let's keep both perspectives. Don't throw one away. If you do that, something remarkable happens. Individual events stop being predictable. Particles follow multiple paths at once. And the only way to make the math consistent is to describe outcomes with complex numbers — that is, with probability amplitudes. In other words, you get quantum behavior not by adding it in, but by refusing to discard the superluminal observer's point of view.

Crucially, their original paper does not claim to explain black holes, dark matter, or the matter-antimatter imbalance. It's a paper about the foundations of quantum theory. But the new computational survey takes their framework and asks: if you take this seriously and build a model with a negative-pressure core, what observables can current or near-future experiments actually measure?

Three channels to catch a fuzzy core

The central idea is concrete: replace the black hole's infinite-density singularity with a finite, negative-pressure "de Sitter" core — the kind that regular-black-hole models by Bardeen, Dymnikova, and Hayward already describe. Then ask: how could we detect it? The calculations reveal three observational channels, each with a different answer.

Channel 1: Ringdown — listening to how a black hole rings

When two black holes merge, the remnant "rings" like a struck bell, emitting gravitational waves at specific frequencies. A regular core shifts those frequencies by a tiny amount. How tiny? For the Hayward model, the shift is $0.049 \times (L/M)^2$, where $L$ is the core size and $M$ is the black hole mass. For the Bardeen model, it's 2.8 times larger: $0.139 \times (e/M)^2$ (WKB-fitted; leading-order analytic estimate 0.110). And for the Dymnikova model, it's essentially zero — the shift is exponentially suppressed, making that type of core completely invisible to ringdown observations, period.

This is a new and surprising result: not all regular black holes are created equal. Hayward cores are subtly detectable. Bardeen cores are more detectable. And Dymnikova cores are radio-silent. The three coefficients span two orders of magnitude in observability — meaning the "regular black hole" label alone does not tell you whether ringdown can find it. You need to know which specific model Nature picked.

The catch: even the most optimistic shift (Bardeen at $L/M = 0.316$, roughly the largest physically reasonable core) is only $|\delta f/f| \approx 1.3\%$ — about ten times smaller than LIGO's current ringdown precision for GW150914. Current detectors can't distinguish a regular black hole from a Schwarzschild one.

But next-generation detectors can. Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer will reach precision of $\sim 0.01\%$, enough to detect a Hayward core as small as $L/M \gtrsim 0.045$ — roughly 2% of the black hole radius. Stacking signals from hundreds of ringdown events in the LIGO O4/O5 era could reach 1% precision sooner, probing $L/M \gtrsim 0.14$.

The isospectrality question: In standard relativity, the two gravitational-wave polarizations from a Schwarzschild black hole ring at exactly the same frequency — a property called isospectrality. Could a regular core break this degeneracy, giving us a second observable? The answer, after careful calculation including black hole spin, is no: axial and polar modes split by less than 0.12% even for large cores. Isospectrality survives. This is a negative result, but a non-trivial consistency check.

Channel 2: Tides — squeezing a neutron star with a hidden core

This is the strongest result in the paper because it connects to data that already exists. The same negative-pressure fluid that smooths out a black hole's interior can also live inside neutron stars. Using the SLy equation of state — a standard nuclear-physics model calibrated to laboratory data — the computer calculation finds:

  • Pure SLy matter produces a maximum neutron star mass of $2.05$ solar masses, consistent with the heaviest known pulsar (PSR J0740+6620, at $2.08 \pm 0.07$).
  • Add even a trace of the hidden core (density above $3 \times 10^{11}$ g/cm³, or about one-tenth of nuclear density) and the maximum mass drops below the observed pulsar — ruled out.
  • The tidal deformability $\Lambda$ — how much a neutron star stretches in a gravitational wave — drops dramatically: from 557 (pure SLy) to $\sim 50$ at high core density. GW170817 already constrains $\Lambda(1.4 M_\odot) \lesssim 580$, and future detectors will measure it to $\sim 1\%$.

A measurement of $\Lambda$ below $\sim 200$ for a $1.4 M_\odot$ neutron star whose companion exceeds $2.0 M_\odot$ would be unexplained by any standard nuclear equation of state — but would be a natural prediction of the hidden-core model. This is a falsifiable claim with existing data.

Post-merger frequency: When two neutron stars merge, the remnant oscillates at a characteristic frequency $f_2 \approx 2 f_{\text{max}}$. The hidden core stiffens the equation of state, shifting $f_2$ upward. For a core density of $\rho_0 = 10^{12}$ g/cm³, the shift is $+27\%$ — well above the $\sim 10\%$ precision expected from the Einstein Telescope. This gives a second, independent test.

The causality check passes. A sweep of 394 different star configurations found zero violations of the rule that sound cannot travel faster than light ($c_s^2 \leq 1$ everywhere). The hidden core actually reduces the sound speed relative to pure nuclear matter — the model is internally consistent despite having no microphysical derivation.

Channel 3: Echoes — a false lead (but an instructive one)

Gravitational-wave echoes — time-delayed pulses from partial reflection between the photon sphere and the core surface — have been widely discussed as a signature of regular black holes. The detailed calculation reveals a problem: echo delays become LIGO-resolvable only at $L/M \gtrsim 0.77$, which coincides exactly with the Hayward extremality condition — the point where the black hole ceases to have a horizon at all.

In plain English: echoes don't test for a regular core inside a black hole. They test for the absence of a black hole entirely. This corrects a genuine confusion in the literature and reframes what echo searches actually constrain. The result is likely generic: the tortoise-coordinate integral that determines echo timing diverges logarithmically near any horizon, meaning resolvable echoes always require near-extremal or horizonless conditions, regardless of the specific regular metric.

The dark matter connection: black holes that never die

If black holes have regular cores, Stephen Hawking's famous calculation gets a new ending. The Hawking temperature does not diverge as the black hole shrinks — it goes to zero. The black hole never fully evaporates. It leaves behind a cold, stable remnant.

The remnant mass can be calculated exactly for the Hayward model: $M_{\text{remnant}} = 3\sqrt{3}/4 \times L \approx 1.3 L$. What this means depends entirely on what $L$ is:

  • If $L$ is the Planck length ($10^{-35}$ m): the remnant is a Planck-scale speck — the standard, unexciting expectation.
  • If $L$ is the QCD scale ($10^{-15}$ m, roughly the size of a proton): the remnant weighs about $10^{15}$ grams — squarely in the mass range where primordial black holes could be the dark matter.
  • If $L$ is any larger: the remnants would already have been seen in microlensing surveys. Existing data (EROS, OGLE, MACHO) push the remnant mass below $10^{18}$ g, corresponding to $L \lesssim 10^{-14}$ m.

This is the single most provocative number in the entire analysis: a direct, calculable bridge between regular black hole physics and the dark matter problem. If $L$ is set by the QCD scale (and there's no currently known reason it should be, but no reason it shouldn't), then evaporated primordial black holes from the early universe would naturally produce the right amount of dark matter.

Three things we've actually figured out since

Since the article was first drafted, several of its explicitly flagged "open problems" have been tackled with concrete calculations:

Charge conjugation: a clever guess, not a derivation

One open problem asked how particle-antiparticle relationships work when you have three time dimensions instead of one. A mathematical argument was constructed, but it's been downgraded from "derivation" to "heuristic postulate" after closer scrutiny. The argument conflates charge conjugation (which flips internal quantum numbers) with time reversal (which flips temporal frequencies). It's still an interesting conjecture, but it's not forced by the Dragan-Ekert kinematics, and a genuine field-theoretic derivation remains open.

The "naturalness" problem for dark matter mixing

If the two observer sectors barely mix (as dark matter requires), what stops quantum fluctuations from driving the mixing back to large values? Short answer: nothing obvious. Graviton loops naturally push the mixing parameter to roughly 0.6% — close, by accident, to the value needed for thermal dark matter production, but ten million times too large for direct detection experiments. The most interesting resolution — that the mixing is a geometric constant, not a tunable coupling — requires a unified group structure that doesn't exist yet.

Two worlds, or two ways of looking at one world? (with a big asterisk)

The article had treated the ordinary and superluminal descriptions as two separate "Hilbert spaces." The answer, it turns out, is that they're the same world seen from two vantage points — like describing the same electron from a frame at rest vs. moving at 0.9c. This was proved in a 1+1-dimensional toy universe. The asterisk: in our actual 3+1-dimensional world, the superluminal transformations don't form a closed group, so the conclusion hasn't been demonstrated in the physically relevant case. For dark matter it's awkward (what makes it "dark" if the other sector is just a different view of the same stuff?); for baryogenesis it's promising, but only if it survives to 3+1D.

Where things stand: a falsifiability table

The analysis is remarkable for being honest about its own limitations. It draws a bright line between established physics (Einstein's relativity, LIGO data), the speculative Dragan-Ekert framework, and the toy hypotheses that are compatible with that framework but not derived from it.

The bottom line

Nobody has proven that faster-than-light observers exist, or that black hole singularities are replaced by smooth cores, or that dark matter consists of cold remnants from evaporated primordial black holes. What the computational analysis offers is a systematic answer to the question: if such cores exist, what would it take to detect them?

The answer turns out to be surprisingly structured. One channel (tidal deformability) is constrained today by GW170817 and NICER. One channel (ringdown) will be constrained in the 2030s by Einstein Telescope and Cosmic Explorer. And one channel (echoes) was a false lead — a category error that the analysis corrects with a clean mathematical coincidence. The Dymnikova result adds an important nuance: some regular cores are observationally invisible. Not all "regular black holes" are created equal, and the word "regular" alone is not enough to make a claim testable. You need to specify which metric.

The single most provocative number in the analysis is $M_{\text{remnant}} \approx 1.3 L$. If the core length scale $L$ is set by the QCD scale — roughly the size of a proton — then cold remnants from the early universe would naturally fall in the mass window where primordial black holes could be the dark matter. This is a direct, falsifiable connection between black hole interiors and cosmology that requires no additional assumptions beyond Hawking's evaporation formalism and the existence of a regular core.

In science, a framework that tells you exactly what measurement would prove it wrong is a framework worth taking seriously — even if it's not yet a framework anyone has proven right.
